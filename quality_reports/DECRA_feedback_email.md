Thanks for sharing the DECRA proposal---the project is tackling an important set of questions, and the institutional access you have through the Fiji Bureau of Statistics is a real strength. The Pacific gap in the climate-wellbeing literature is genuine, and Fiji is a well-chosen setting to fill it. I have some suggestions that I think would strengthen the proposal considerably, focused on the areas where ARC reviewers are most likely to push back.

The most important revision is to add a conceptual framework. Right now the proposal asserts that "climate shocks alter household budget constraints, increase income volatility, and reshape expectations about future returns to labour and investment" (P6) but never develops this into a framework that generates testable predictions. The problem is that without a model, opposite empirical results can both be rationalised ex post. Take migration: does a cyclone that destroys assets tighten the liquidity constraint and trap people in place, or does it lower the option value of staying and push them toward migration? The proposal flags exactly this tension---"whether mobility intentions reflect distress, adaptation, or forward-looking investment behaviour" (P8)---but doesn't develop the apparatus to distinguish these stories. Even a simple two-period household optimisation setup would discipline the empirics, reduce the impression of an unfocused outcome list, and show reviewers you have priors about signs, magnitudes, and heterogeneity patterns.

The identification strategy needs tightening in several places. The core identifying assumption (P28) conflates spatial variation in wind speed and rainfall across villages with temporal variation in temperature from survey timing---these are different sources of variation with different confound structures, and reviewers will notice. The relationship between the continuous fixed-effects model (equation 1) and the binary DiD design also needs clarification: these are different estimands answering different questions, and the proposal should be explicit about which is primary. The Goodman-Bacon (2021) citation is a diagnostic for staggered TWFE, not an estimator---you should specify which modern DiD estimator you plan to use (Callaway and Sant'Anna, Sun and Abraham, etc.) and explain why the DiD adds something the continuous specification cannot provide.

A critical gap is the absence of any discussion of selective migration. If cyclones cause people to move---which is one of your key outcomes---then the panel loses precisely the most-affected individuals, biasing the remaining sample. This needs to be addressed head-on, whether through tracking protocols, bounding exercises (Lee bounds), or at minimum an honest discussion of the threat. More broadly, the proposal would benefit from a dedicated threats section covering spillovers through kinship networks in a small-island economy, confounding from government and NGO disaster response, anticipation effects from cyclone forecasting, and multiple-testing corrections across the nine outcome families.

On data and power: with 2,000 households and four waves over two years, you're looking at perhaps one to three cyclone events during the collection window. The proposal needs power calculations or at least a discussion of minimum detectable effects, especially given that you plan subgroup analyses. Relatedly, constructing a new wellbeing measure from wave-1 data and then using the same sample for causal estimation creates a circularity concern---consider whether validation can use a held-out sample, or whether you'll also report results using standard internationally validated measures as a benchmark.

Finally, two framing issues. Seven peer-reviewed articles targeting A*-ranked outlets including the QJE is ambitious to the point where it may undermine credibility with reviewers. A tighter plan---three or four well-identified papers with realistic journal targets---would be more convincing and signal focus. And while the geopolitical framing around Australia's strategic interests is policy-relevant, it currently crowds out space that would be better spent on the economic reasoning and identification strategy. The China reference (P5) in particular is unlikely to persuade an economics review panel and could be cut entirely to make room for a conceptual framework or threats discussion.

The core of this proposal---causal evidence on climate shocks and wellbeing in a Pacific Island setting, using a purpose-built panel survey---is strong and fundable. The revisions above are about sharpening the presentation to match the quality of the underlying research design. Happy to discuss any of these points further.
