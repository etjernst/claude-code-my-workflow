[P1] C1	PROJECT DESCRIPTION

[P2] Project Title: Beyond Income: Climate Risk, Wellbeing, And Economic Behaviour

[P3] PROJECT QUALITY AND INNOVATION:

[P4] Contribution of the aim to an important gap in knowledge and to address a significant problem:

[P5] Australia produces just over 1% of total global greenhouse gas emissions, ranking as the world's 14th largest emitter. However, over many years, Australia has had one of the highest per-capita emissions in the world—around 15.4 tonnes per person, which is roughly three times higher than the global average, double that of China and nine times that of India [1]. Pacific Island Countries (PICs), located in close proximity to Australia, are among the most climate-exposed regions globally. Intensifying cyclones, flooding, and coastal inundation threaten not only income and physical assets, but also the wellbeing and long-run economic trajectories of households living under persistent environmental vulnerability [2]. At the same time, the Pacific has become a region of heightened international geopolitical engagement, with increasing development finance, infrastructure investment, and strategic partnerships from external actors. For Australia, whose security, economic interests, and social cohesion are closely linked to regional stability, doing sound causal analysis on a solid empirical evidence base on how climate shocks affect wellbeing and economic behaviour in neighbouring Pacific states is both a development and strategic security priority. Fiji, as the largest and most economically diversified Pacific Island economy and one of the major sources of regional labour mobility, occupies a position of regional leadership and represents a strategically significant case through which to understand these dynamics. What is happening to Fiji’s subjective wellbeing in the face of these challenges? Climate change is exogenously affecting employment and health outcomes of Fijians and their necessity to migrate to neighbouring countries. The project will establish an evidence base with which to analyse these outcomes to inform Australian and Fijian policymakers and employers with actionable insights that support the effective integration of Pacific peoples whose expanding labour mobility and migration pathways are increasingly reshaping Australian communities and labour markets. Further, Australia has a historical responsibility to deal with the fallout of its own industrial polluting behaviour, if it wants to avoid other powers such as China gaining a political and economic foothold on Australia’s back door.

[P6] The Australian Government is pursuing deeper Australia–Pacific integration and has responded to calls from Pacific leaders for easier regional mobility through the Pacific Engagement Visa (PEV) [3]. In announcing the program, the news and media article page of the department stated that “that boosting Pacific permanent migration to Australia is an essential part of our plan to build a stronger Pacific Family to grow Australia's Pacific diaspora.” This policy context raises two strategic questions. First, as a major development partner in the region, how should Australia anticipate and respond to climate-driven vulnerability while supporting resilience in Pacific states? Second, as strategic competition in the Pacific intensifies, how can Australia ensure its climate, development, and mobility policies strengthen regional partnerships through credible, evidence-based engagement? Both imperatives require rigorous empirical evidence on how climate shocks affect wellbeing and economic behaviour. From an economics perspective, climate shocks alter household budget constraints, increase income volatility, and reshape expectations about future returns to labour and investment. Without robust evidence on this mechanisms, policy risks relying on assumptions rather than the lived drivers of Pacific household decision making.

[P7] Fiji, home to around 900,000 people and located just 3.5 hours from Brisbane, occupies a central position in Australia–Pacific relations as a Commonwealth partner and longstanding migration corridor. Fijians have migrated to Australia through pathways such as the Skilled Independent visa (subclass 189), and more recently, the Pacific Engagement Visa (subclass 192). At the same time, Fiji faces recurrent tropical cyclones, flooding, and coastal inundation, risks that are intensifying under climate change [4]. While Australia has recognised existential climate risks in the Pacific such as through its commitment to Tuvalu under the Australia–Tuvalu Falepili Union [5], climate pressures in Fiji may shape migration decisions in more gradual but economically significant ways. Understanding whether climate shocks erode wellbeing and influence migration intentions, participation in labour mobility schemes (including the Pacific Australia Labour Mobility (PALM) scheme), or long-term relocation decisions is therefore critical for both Fijian policy and Australian migration, labour market, and foreign policy planning.

[P8] The aim of this project is to generate robust causal evidence on how climate shocks affect individual wellbeing and shape economic and social behaviour in vulnerable economies. Exploiting quasi-experimental variation in climate exposure and high-quality survey data, the project aims to identify the causal impacts of climate shocks on subjective wellbeing including life satisfaction, financial wellbeing, and mental health as well as economic outcomes (employment, migration intentions, financial risk-taking) and social outcomes (social cohesion, community trust, and kinship networks). Understanding these causal pathways is critical for Australia. As Pacific labour mobility expands and Pacific diasporas assume a growing role in Australia’s labour markets and regional communities, climate-induced changes in wellbeing and economic behaviour in source countries may influence the scale, timing, and composition of migration flows, labour supply responses, and remittance behaviour. These dynamics carry downstream implications for workforce planning, regional development, and integration outcomes in Australia. By identifying whether mobility intentions reflect distress, adaptation, or forward-looking investment behaviour, the project will provide evidence essential for designing effective climate adaptation support, labour mobility frameworks, and community integration policies that align with both Australia’s economic interests and its regional stability objectives.

[P9] Hence, this project addresses a significant gap in knowledge and responds to a pressing policy challenge in the following four ways. First, the project addresses a critical empirical gap at the intersection of climate risk, wellbeing economics, and development policy. While Australia has long supported Fiji’s climate resilience efforts, assessing value-for-money remains incomplete without understanding impacts through a wellbeing lens. Existing research shows that climate change has persistent effects on economic output ([6], [7]) and health [8] with effects that are often unequal and disproportionately borne by low-income and climate-exposed regions ([2],[9],[10]).  Yet evidence on the broader wellbeing consequences of climate shocks particularly in Pacific Island contexts remains limited. This gap is especially salient in Fiji, where high climate exposure and strong social and community structures shape how households experience and respond to environmental risk.

[P10] Second, the project advances the conceptualisation and measurement of wellbeing in small, climate vulnerable economies. While drawing on existing state-of-the-art subjective wellbeing instruments ([11], [12], [13]), it will test their sensitivity to environmental risk and economic behaviour, while also developing and validating a Fiji-specific multidimensional wellbeing measure grounded in local socioeconomic and cultural context. As no known existing studies jointly assess multidimensional and single-item wellbeing measures in Pacific Island settings, Fiji provides a unique natural field context to compare these approaches and evaluate how different constructs capture vulnerability and adaptation under climate risk.

[P11] Third, the project applies rigorous quasi-causal econometric methods to identify how climate shocks reshape economic and social behaviour. It will examine how climate-induced shocks affects employment, migration intentions, financial risk-taking and shift social cohesion, community trust and strength of kinship networks. By moving beyond documenting welfare losses to identifying behavioural responses, the project generates new knowledge on how households adapt and build resilience in vulnerable economies.

[P12] Fourth, the project reconceptualises climate risk as a cross-border economic phenomenon. As labour mobility and migration pathways between Australia and Fiji deepens, climate-related shifts in wellbeing, economic outcomes, and social cohesion in Fiji are not confined domestically; they influence migration intentions, remittance flows, and by downstream implications community integration outcomes within Australia. By analysing behavioural indicators such as passport uptake, engagement with labour recruiters, and applications to the Pacific Engagement Visa (PEV) ballot in Fiji, this project in informs forward-looking migration and workforce policy. In doing so, it strengthens the evidence base underpinning the Australia–Fiji Vuvale Partnership [14] and supports climate-resilient development in both Australia and Fiji.

[P13] Research innovation in the context of recent international advances in research in this area:

[P14] This project is innovative for at least three reasons. First, it advances for the first time wellbeing measurement by developing and validating a Fiji-specific metric that is both contextually grounded and internationally comparable. While international wellbeing comparisons such as the World Happiness Report rely on Gallup World Poll life-evaluation data from more than 140 countries, Fiji is not currently reported in the published country rankings. At the same time, Fiji does not yet have a validated, nationally representative wellbeing framework grounded in internationally recognised subjective wellbeing measures, leaving an important gap in both global comparability and domestic policy measurement. Building on emerging regional efforts including Vanuatu’s participation in a global wellbeing measurement initiative [15] and earlier work in Tuvalu [16], this project develops and psychometrically validates a Fiji-specific measure that maintains global conventions while ensuring contextual and cultural relevance, thereby strengthening the measurement infrastructure for wellbeing in the Pacific.

[P15] Second, the project shifts Pacific wellbeing research from descriptive framework development to causal identification. The validated wellbeing measure will be embedded within a quasi-experimental econometric framework that links geocoded cyclone tracks, wind intensity, and rainfall variation to household-level outcomes. Exploiting spatial and temporal heterogeneity in exposure, the study estimates short- and medium-term impacts of climate shocks on wellbeing, economic behaviour (employment, migration intentions, financial risk-taking), and social outcomes (cohesion, trust, kinship networks), including heterogeneity across socio-economic groups. This represents a methodological advance in climate economics within small-state settings, where rigorous causal evidence remains limited.

[P16] Third, the project reconceptualises climate risk as an international cross-border economic phenomenon. Rather than treating climate impacts as a problem confined to a single nation, this project recognises the cross-border externalities they generate by empirically examining how climate-induced changes in wellbeing and economic outcomes in Fiji shape migration intentions, labour supply decisions, and broader bilateral economic linkages with Australia. By modelling these behavioural pathways, the research will generate novel evidence on how environmental shocks in vulnerable small states reshape regional labour markets and economic integration, directly informing forward looking migration and regional engagement policy.

[P17] Major research questions:

[P18] The project will answer three research questions:

[P19] How is wellbeing conceptualised beyond income, and which dimensions matter most in Fiji?

[P20] In addressing this research question, the project will draw on learnings from economics literature ([11], [12], [13], [17], [18]) and policy documents ([19], [20], [21]) focusing on measures and conceptualisation of wellbeing and apply it in the context of Fiji. The choice between single-item and multi-item measures, and the selection of domains, remain debatable and context dependent. This project will revisit the existing literature and critically assess wellbeing measures to identify those most appropriate for the Fijian context. Wellbeing dimension selection will be informed through structured stakeholder consultations to ensure validity, cultural relevance, and policy alignment. This analysis is expected to generate two peer-reviewed academic articles for submission to high-quality ABDC-ranked journals.

[P21] What is the causal effect of climate shocks on subjective wellbeing and related economic and social behaviours for individuals and across communities?

[P22] In answering this research question, the project seeks to draw on theories on climate shocks and exposure and their implications ([2], [7], [10], [22], [23]). Recent studies show that environmental conditions and climate-related shocks can have large and persistent effects on wellbeing, often exceeding their impacts on income or employment alone, particularly in low- and middle-income and climate-vulnerable settings. Leveraging this literature, the project will combine individual-level wellbeing data with spatially and temporally matched rainfall and temperature measures in Fiji to estimate the short-run effects of climate on wellbeing. By exploiting variation across individuals and communities (‘tikina’), the analysis will identify how climate shocks translate into differential wellbeing losses, highlighting impacts that are not captured by traditional economic indicators. The project will also empirically test how climate shocks transmit to individual economic behaviour and social outcomes. This analysis is expected to generate three peer-reviewed academic articles for submission to high-quality ABDC-ranked journals.

[P23] Do the wellbeing effects of climate shocks dissipate over time or accumulate through repeated exposure?

[P24] Another key question is whether individuals adapt to adverse shocks or experience persistent welfare losses. Empirical studies in this strand of literature show partial adaptation in some domains such as [24] document lasting wellbeing effects to disability while [7] highlight that temperature shocks have long-run growth consequences, particularly in poorer countries. However, there is no known empirical evidence that exists for climate vulnerable Pacific-island economies. This project tests whether subjective wellbeing in Fiji exhibits recovery following climate shocks or whether repeated exposure generates cumulative scarring effects. This analysis is expected to generate two peer-reviewed academic articles for submission to high-quality ABDC-ranked journals.

[P25] Project design, including conceptual framework and methods:

[P26] Construction of wellbeing measure for Fiji: The project integrates advances in economics and psychological science with national policy frameworks to develop a contextually grounded wellbeing measure for Fiji. It will combine a global single-item life satisfaction measure with a multidimensional scale capturing satisfaction across key domains: standard of living, health, relationships, safety, community belonging (including to vanua), future security, and religiosity. In recognition of Fiji’s environmental vulnerability, additional domains such as climate security and attachment to land will be explicitly tested for inclusion. Instrument development will involve structured engagement with key agencies, including the Ministry of Strategic Planning, National Development and Statistics; the Ministry of Environment and Climate Change; and the Fiji Bureau of Statistics (FBoS) as well as consultations with Community village leaders, women’s groups, and youth representatives to ensure cultural validity and policy alignment. An Academic Technical Advisory Panel comprised of local and international academics will also be formed to provide methodological oversight to ensure theoretical coherence and adherence to best-practice survey standards. The multidimensional measure will undergo rigorous psychometric validation, including factor analysis and reliability testing with a dedicated pilot study prior to implementation in a nationally representative survey.

[P27] Conceptual framework: The project design is based on the framework that links geocoded cyclone tracks, wind intensity, rainfall and temperature variation to household-level outcomes. The meteorological data on cyclones, wind and rainfall will produce sufficient variation with which to identify the causal outcomes/effects. These shocks are exogenous to households and communities conditional on fixed effects and represent the primary source of variation used for causal identification. In the context of Fiji, these shocks are frequent and heterogeneous across space and time, making them particularly well suited for empirical analysis.

[P28] The effects of climate exposure on wellbeing and related behavioural outcomes are identified by exploiting spatial and temporal variation in rainfall, temperature and wind speed across climatically homogeneous zones in Fiji. The core identifying assumption is that variation in wind speed and rainfall intensity within narrowly defined climate zones is exogenous. That is, we assume that individuals within the same climate zone experience variation in temperature because of differences in survey timing, rather than persistent spatial differences in climatic or socioeconomic conditions [22]. Our empirical specification will be estimated as a fixed-effects model that relates the outcome to contemporaneous climate exposure, controlling for time-invariant individual heterogeneity and common time shocks:

First, to allow for non-linear responses, temperature, wind speed, and rainfall exposure will be modelled using discrete bins and threshold specifications, enabling the identification of potential tipping points for the effects. [P32] The project will also employ recent advances in the Difference-in-Differences (DiD) such as the [25] framework to strengthen causal identification. Specifically, wellbeing, economic, and social outcomes of individuals residing within defined proximity to cyclone tracks or flood zones (treatment group) will be compared to those in less-affected areas (control group), before and after the shock. Treatment intensity will be measured using geocoded wind speed and rainfall data. Control groups will be constructed using two complementary strategies: (i) households located outside high-exposure zones within Fiji, and (ii) matched regions with similar pre-shock socio-economic characteristics to ensure comparability and mitigate selection bias.

[P33] Data sources:

[P34] Household-level data will be collected through a nationally representative survey of approximately 2,000 households, sampled using probability proportional to size from the most recent Fiji Census frame, in close collaboration with the Fiji Bureau of Statistics (FBoS). Sampled households will be followed over four survey waves at six-month intervals, generating a longitudinal panel dataset. This design enables the estimation of dynamic effects of climate shocks on wellbeing and related economic and social outcomes, while controlling for unobserved household-level heterogeneity through fixed-effects models. The sampling strategy ensures broad geographic and socio-economic representation across Fiji, spanning gender, location and age groups. Formal letters of support have been received from the Acting Chief Executive of the Fiji Bureau of Statistics, Mr Apisalome Tamani, confirming Bureau’s willingness to facilitate data collection and engagement, thereby strengthening the project’s feasibility and implementation capacity.

[P35] Fiji Meteorological Services upon request offers detailed daily climate related data at community ‘tikina’ level which will be used to capture rainfall, temperature, wind speed and cyclone tracks if there is one. These data will be complemented by global satellite weather data available from the National Aeronautics and Space Administration’s (NASA) MERRA-2 dataset. Its primary use will be in filling gaps in station coverage, supplying additional variables, or cross-validating national data.

[P48] BENEFIT:

[P49] New and advanced knowledge from the research:

[P50] This project generates frontier evidence in climate economics and welfare analysis in vulnerable small island economies. First, it will provide the first causal estimates of the welfare, economic, and social impacts of climate shocks in Fiji using quasi-experimental variation in weather exposure. Second, it advances wellbeing measurement by comparing multidimensional and single-item frameworks under climate stress. Third, it examines transnational spillovers, identifying how climate-induced welfare shocks influence migration and labour market linkages with Australia.

[P51] Potential benefits for Australia and/or international communities of the research:

[P52] This research delivers clear national and international benefits by strengthening Australia’s economic resilience, regional stability, and climate adaptation strategy in the Pacific. By generating rigorous causal evidence on how climate shocks affect wellbeing and economic behaviour in Fiji - one of Australia’s closest regional partners - it addresses a critical evidence gap at the intersection of climate risk, migration, and economic outcomes.

[P53] Economically, the findings will inform labour market and migration policy by identifying how climate-induced changes in wellbeing, employment prospects, and economic security in Fiji may influence migration intentions, labour supply decisions, and remittance flows, thereby improving workforce planning and bilateral economic integration. Environmentally, the project moves beyond GDP-based metrics to incorporate welfare and behavioural effects into climate impact assessment, strengthening cost-benefit analysis and value-for-money evaluation of adaptation investments. Socially and strategically, it provides insights into social cohesion and resilience under climate stress, supporting climate-resilient societies in the Pacific. To the research communities, the creation of a high-quality, internationally comparable dataset will further strengthen Australia–Pacific research partnerships and deliver sustained public-good benefits beyond the duration of the existing proposed project. The project aligns directly with Australian Government National Science and Research Priorities, including “Securing healthy and thriving communities” and “Building a secure and resilient nation.”

[P54] COMMUNICATION OF RESULTS

[P55] The findings of this research will be disseminated through a multi-tiered strategy targeting academic, policy, regional, and public audiences.

[P56] Academically, the project will produce seven peer-reviewed journal articles submitted to leading ABDC A* journals in environmental and development and applied economics (such as The Quarterly Journal of Economics, Journal of Development Economics, The Economic Journal, Journal of Labor Economics, Journal of Economic Behaviour and Organisation, Journal of Environment Economics and Management). Findings will be presented at major field and policy relevant conferences, including at the Australian Conference of Economists, Australian AIDS conference, International Society for Quality-of-Life Studies Conference and the Pacific Update Conference, ensuring both international visibility and regional engagement.

[P57] Policy engagement will include project launch events in Melbourne and Suva with key Australian and Fijian stakeholders. In Australia, platforms such as the Public Economics Forum organised by the Melbourne Institute will provide a channel for direct engagement with Canberra-based policymakers, government departments, and academic experts. In Fiji, targeted policy roundtables will be convened in collaboration with the Fiji Bureau of Statistics and relevant ministries, including those responsible for Strategic Planning, Employment, Foreign Affairs, and Environment and Climate Change. These forums will translate research findings into policy-relevant dialogue, ensuring the project informs national debates on climate adaptation and wellbeing. In addition, a public webinar hosted by the Melbourne Institute and promoted through its communications and media channels will be delivered to disseminate findings to a broad audience, ensuring accessible engagement for those unable to attend in person.

[P58] Third, a dedicated interactive data dashboard will be developed to host survey-based wellbeing data and associated outputs in an accessible, user-friendly format. The platform will provide dynamic visualisations and interactive summaries that enhance transparency, support evidence-informed policymaking, and facilitate engagement with government and community stakeholders. It will also enable structured access to key indicators, supporting ongoing research collaboration and maximising the project’s long-term public value.

[P59] Finally, non-technical summaries will be disseminated through University of Melbourne channels, departmental websites, and public-facing outlets such as The Conversation and national newspapers, ensuring broad accessibility while maintaining academic rigour.
